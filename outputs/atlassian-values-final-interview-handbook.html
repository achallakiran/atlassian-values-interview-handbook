Interview Strategy

What Atlassian Is Evaluating

They are not only checking whether you can recite values. They are checking how you behave when delivery pressure, ambiguity, customer impact, technical debt, and people dynamics collide. For a Senior Engineering Manager / Principal Data Engineering role, the bar is: can you create clarity, raise engineering quality, protect customers, develop leaders, and influence outside your reporting line without becoming political or defensive?

Your positioning: "I lead large data engineering teams through complex platform work by combining technical depth, stakeholder clarity, and calm operating discipline. I care about customer outcomes, but I also care about the engineering system that produces those outcomes repeatedly."

How Behavioural Interviews Work

The interviewer listens for evidence. One specific story beats five abstract claims. They are mapping your story to judgement, self-awareness, ownership, collaboration, and learning. They will often push on tradeoffs: "What did you do personally?", "What would you do differently?", "How did you know it worked?", "How did the team feel?"

STAR Framework

  • Situation: one or two lines of context. Do not over-explain architecture.
  • Task: your leadership responsibility, not just the project goal.
  • Action: the richest part. Include decisions, tradeoffs, communication, coaching, and operating mechanisms.
  • Result: concrete but believable. Use directionally honest outcomes: fewer incidents, clearer ownership, faster onboarding, smoother cutover, better stakeholder trust.

Answer Length

A strong final-round values answer is usually 90-120 seconds. Use 15 seconds for context, 15 seconds for the challenge, 55-75 seconds for actions, and 15-25 seconds for results and learning. If the story is complex, say: "I'll keep the technical detail light unless helpful."

Common Mistakes

  • Sounding like the hero who saved everyone instead of the leader who built a stronger system.
  • Over-indexing on technical implementation and under-explaining stakeholder impact.
  • Describing conflict as "alignment issues" without naming the real tension.
  • Claiming customer obsession while only measuring platform metrics.
  • Not explaining what you personally did versus what the team did.

Good Phrases To Use

  • "I tried to separate urgency from importance."
  • "I made the tradeoff explicit instead of letting it stay implicit."
  • "I wanted the team to own the decision, not just comply with it."
  • "The customer impact was indirect, but real."
  • "I used data to create shared reality."
  • "My role was to create clarity and remove friction."
  • "I took accountability for the miss and changed the operating model."
  • "I did not want reliability to depend on tribal knowledge."

Phrases To Avoid

  • "I convinced everyone."
  • "The business did not understand engineering."
  • "I escalated and got it done."
  • "It was just a communication issue."
  • "My team worked weekends."
  • "We had no choice."
  • "I handled everything myself."
  • "The legacy system was bad."

Chapter 1: Play, as a Team

What It Really Means

What Interviewer Is Testing

Can you collaborate without losing accountability? Can you disagree respectfully, share credit, create operating rhythm, and lead through influence when priorities conflict?

Strong Signals

  • You built cross-team mechanisms.
  • You resolved ownership ambiguity.
  • You coached engineers to collaborate directly.
  • You used shared metrics, not status theatre.

Weak Signals

Chapter 4: Open Company, No Bullsh*t

What It Really Means

Be direct, transparent, and respectful. Surface bad news early. Do not hide behind status colors, vague language, or hierarchy. Use truth to help the team make better decisions.

What Interviewer Is Testing

Can you have difficult conversations upward, sideways, and downward? Do you create psychological safety while still holding a high bar?

Strong Signals

  • You named risk before it became failure.
  • You gave hard feedback with care.
  • You admitted your own miss.
  • You created factual, blameless postmortems.

Weak Signals

  • Being "blunt" without empathy.
  • Using transparency as complaint.
  • Surprising stakeholders late.
  • Not showing what changed after the truth was shared.

Top 5 Questions

  2. Tell me about a time you gave tough feedback to an engineer.
  3. Tell me about a time you admitted a mistake.
  4. How do you handle bad news in a high-visibility program?
  5. Describe a time you disagreed with leadership.

1. Tell me about a time you led a cross-functional initiative with conflicting priorities.

Question

1. Tell me about a time you led a cross-functional initiative with conflicting priorities.

Why interviewer asks it

They want to know whether you can align teams without formal authority and still deliver.

Which Atlassian value it maps to

Play, as a Team; Open Company, No Bullsh*t.

Recommended Story

Multi-country data platform migration with Product, Architects, TPMs, country engineering teams, and downstream consumers.

STAR Answer

Situation

In one migration program, we were moving critical data pipelines to a more modern GCP-based platform across multiple countries. Each country had different reporting deadlines, legacy dependencies, and tolerance for change.

Task

My responsibility was to lead the engineering plan for my 18-person team while keeping Product, architecture, TPM, and country stakeholders aligned on sequencing and risk.

Action

I started by making the tradeoffs visible. Instead of debating dates in every meeting, we created a migration readiness scorecard: data volume, downstream dependencies, SLA criticality, rollback readiness, and business calendar risk. I then split the work into waves. Low-risk domains moved first, which let us harden frameworks and monitoring before touching high-impact datasets. For disagreements, I used a simple rule: if the risk was customer or business-facing, we decided with the impacted stakeholders in the room. If it was implementation detail, engineering owned it. I also asked senior engineers to pair directly with country teams so the relationship was not only manager-to-manager.

Result

The program became calmer and more predictable. We still had hard tradeoffs, but they were explicit. Stakeholders could see why something moved earlier or later, and the engineering team had fewer priority resets. My main learning was that cross-functional alignment is not one big meeting; it is a shared operating model.

Likely Follow-up Questions

  • What was the hardest disagreement?
  • How did you handle a country team that resisted?
  • What did you personally do versus delegate?
  • How did you measure migration success?

Excellent Closing Sentence

"The biggest win was not just completing the migration; it was creating a decision system everyone trusted."

Common Mistakes

  • Making it sound like stakeholder management was status reporting.
  • Over-explaining GCP architecture.
  • Claiming everyone aligned immediately.

Interview Tips

  • Name the conflict clearly.
  • Show your operating mechanism.
  • Give shared credit.

Recommended Story

Reusable data engineering framework for ingestion, validation, logging, alerts, and deployment standards.

STAR Answer

Situation

As our data platform grew, teams were solving similar problems differently: ingestion patterns, retry logic, data validation, schema changes, and operational alerts. It worked when the team was small, but it created uneven quality and too much tribal knowledge.

Task

I wanted to improve engineering consistency without slowing teams down with heavy process.

Action

I asked two senior engineers to audit recurring defects and operational pain points from the previous few quarters. We found that many issues came from missing validation, inconsistent logging, and unclear ownership during failures. I sponsored a lightweight reusable framework in Python and Scala patterns, with templates for Spark jobs, BigQuery loads, validation checks, runbooks, and alerting. I was careful not to mandate it blindly. We piloted it on a few new pipelines, collected feedback, simplified the interface, and then made it the default for new work. I also changed code review expectations: reviewers were no longer only checking logic; they checked operability.

Result

New pipelines became easier to review and support. Onboarding improved because engineers had a known pattern. We reduced repeat operational issues and had better confidence during releases. The important part was that quality became part of how we delivered, not a separate cleanup initiative.

Likely Follow-up Questions

  • How did you get engineers to adopt it?
  • Did anyone resist?
  • How did you avoid over-standardizing?
  • What metrics changed?

Excellent Closing Sentence

"I wanted quality to be built into the path of least resistance."

Common Mistakes

  • Ignoring engineer adoption.
  • Not tying quality to customer trust.

3. Tell me about a production incident or data quality issue.

Question

3. Tell me about a production incident or data quality issue.

Why interviewer asks it

They want ownership, transparency, and customer-first judgement under pressure.

Which Atlassian value it maps to

Don't #@!% the Customer; Open Company, No Bullsh*t.

Recommended Story

Incorrect or delayed downstream dataset affecting business reporting, caused by schema drift or late upstream feed.

STAR Answer

Situation

We had a case where a critical downstream dataset used by business teams became unreliable after an upstream schema change was not caught early enough. The pipeline did not completely fail; it produced partial data, which is often worse because consumers may not realize the issue immediately.

Task

My job was to protect the consumers, restore trust in the dataset, and make sure we did not treat it as a one-off bug.

Action

First, we stopped the spread of bad data and notified the impacted stakeholders with what we knew, what we did not know, and when we would update them next. I asked the team to validate the last known good partition and provide a clear recommendation on whether users should pause decisions based on that dataset. Then we fixed the pipeline and backfilled the affected period. After recovery, I ran a blameless review focused on detection gaps. We added schema validation, data freshness checks, anomaly thresholds, and clearer ownership between upstream and downstream teams. I also changed our release checklist for pipelines serving high-visibility reporting.

Result

The immediate issue was resolved, but more importantly, we improved early detection and communication. Stakeholders appreciated that we were direct rather than defensive. My takeaway was that data reliability is not only about uptime; it is about whether customers can confidently make decisions.

Likely Follow-up Questions

  • How fast did you communicate?
  • Who owned the upstream dependency?
  • What would you do differently?
  • How did you prevent recurrence?

Excellent Closing Sentence

"For data platforms, silent correctness issues are the incidents I take most seriously."

Common Mistakes

  • Minimizing the customer impact.
  • Blaming upstream teams.
  • Only discussing technical fix.

Interview Tips

  • Stress transparency.
  • Use "partial data is dangerous."
  • Close with systemic prevention.

We met the business need without forcing risky datasets through the migration. Would you escalate?

I escalate when the decision affects customer trust or business risk and the right owners are not aligned. But I try to escalate with options, not just with a complaint.

5. Proactive governance follow-ups
How did you avoid bureaucracy?

I tied governance to daily engineering pain: faster incident routing, clearer access decisions, safer migrations, and better dataset trust. Anything that did not help those outcomes was kept out.

Who resisted?

Some teams worried it would become extra documentation work. I handled that by starting with critical datasets only and using lightweight templates rather than asking everyone to fill long forms.

How did you measure adoption?

I looked at coverage for critical datasets, owner completeness, SLA metadata, quality checks, and whether incidents routed faster. Adoption had to show up in operations, not just documents.

How did Product react?

Product was supportive once the work was framed as customer confidence and roadmap predictability. Governance became easier to prioritize when it reduced surprises for consumers.

6. Deadline versus quality follow-ups
Who made the final decision?

I made the engineering recommendation and brought the business tradeoff to the right stakeholders. For customer-impacting scope, I wanted Product and business owners involved, but I owned the technical risk assessment.

How did you communicate the partial release?

I communicated it as a scoped release, not a delay. The message was: here is what is safe to move, here is what needs more validation, and here are the exit criteria for the next wave.

What quality bar was non-negotiable?

For critical datasets, reconciliation, rollback, observability, and owner sign-off were non-negotiable. We could adjust scope, but we could not knowingly create silent data correctness risk.

How did you avoid scope creep?

We defined exit criteria upfront and separated must-fix validation gaps from nice-to-have improvements. Anything not tied to the release risk went into the follow-up backlog.

7. Coaching follow-ups
How did you give the feedback?

I gave it privately and specifically. I told the engineer their technical judgement was strong, but their next level of impact required making others effective through design clarity, delegation, and mentoring.

What if they disagreed?

I would ask for their view and compare it against role expectations and examples. The goal is not to win the conversation; it is to create shared clarity on what senior leadership looks like.

How did you measure growth?

I looked for whether others could execute from their designs, whether they delegated meaningful work, whether cross-team partners trusted them, and whether I was less of a bottleneck.

How do you scale coaching across 18 engineers?

I use a mix of direct one-on-ones, lead-level coaching, design reviews, feedback calibration, and giving senior engineers explicit mentoring responsibilities. Coaching cannot depend only on me.

8. Tough feedback follow-ups
How did they react?

Initially they were a bit defensive, which is normal. The conversation improved once I made clear that the feedback was about senior expectations and that I was giving them a concrete path to succeed.

What if they did not improve?

I would tighten expectations, document the gap, increase feedback frequency, and align with HR/leadership if needed. Care does not mean avoiding accountability.

How do you document feedback?

I summarize the expectation, examples, agreed actions, and review date. Documentation should create clarity for both sides, not feel like a surprise file being built later.

How do you avoid demotivating people?

I separate behavior from identity and pair candor with belief. People can usually handle hard feedback if it is specific, fair, and connected to growth.

9. Influence without authority follow-ups
Who resisted?

Peer teams resisted when they saw the standard as extra work or as my team's preference. I reduced that by co-designing the standard and showing incident evidence from across teams.

Did leadership sponsor it?

Eventually yes, but I did not wait for sponsorship to start. I used a pilot and evidence first, then leadership sponsorship helped scale it.

How did you handle non-adoption?

I focused on critical datasets first. If a team chose not to adopt, I asked them to show an equivalent control for quality, ownership, and escalation rather than insisting on my exact implementation.

How did you measure success?

By adoption coverage, fewer repeated incidents, better owner clarity, and faster incident routing. Influence was successful only if it changed operating behavior.

10. Disagreeing with leadership follow-ups
What if leadership said no?

I would document the risk, make sure the decision owner understood the impact, and then execute professionally with the strongest guardrails possible. Disagreeing does not remove my responsibility to deliver.

How did you avoid sounding negative?

I started by supporting the business goal, then challenged the execution risk. I also brought alternatives, which made it a problem-solving conversation rather than a blocker conversation.

Did this affect your relationship?

It improved trust over time because I was direct and evidence-based. Leaders usually appreciate disagreement when it protects the outcome and does not become personal.

How do you know when to escalate?

I escalate when the risk crosses team boundaries, affects customers, or requires a tradeoff my team should not make alone. I do not escalate routine implementation disagreements.

11. Roadmap and customer feedback follow-ups
What did you deprioritize?

We slowed a few internal platform enhancements that were useful but less urgent than customer trust and discoverability. I made that tradeoff explicit with Product.

How did you collect feedback?

I used direct consumer conversations, support patterns, repeated questions, adoption data, and incident learnings. For platform teams, support friction is often customer feedback in disguise.

How did you know it helped?

We saw fewer repeated clarification requests, cleaner onboarding for consumers, and better confidence in migration readiness. The best signal was that users could self-serve more decisions.

How did Product participate?

Product helped prioritize the user pain, communicate tradeoffs, and define what mattered for adoption. I owned the engineering plan, but Product helped keep us honest on customer value.

12. Admitting a mistake follow-ups
How did stakeholders react?

They were frustrated but appreciated the clarity. Owning the communication gap helped move the conversation from blame to how we would prevent surprises in the next wave.

What was the cost of the mistake?

The cost was mainly trust and rework, not a catastrophic outage. That still mattered because migrations depend on stakeholder confidence as much as technical execution.

How did your team respond?

The team responded well because I did not push the blame downward. We treated it as a leadership and operating-model improvement, then updated the readiness process together.

What did you change permanently?

Customer readiness became part of migration readiness. Updates had to explain impact, decisions, dates, rollback criteria, and contacts, not just task status.

13. Customer obsession follow-ups
Who are your customers?

For my teams, customers include business operators, analysts, product teams, data scientists, country teams, and downstream platforms. They may be internal, but their decisions affect external customers and business outcomes.

What metrics do you use?

I use freshness, correctness, SLA adherence, incident rate, time to detect, time to recover, adoption, support volume, and consumer confidence signals. Job success alone is not enough.

How do you prioritize customer asks?

I look at customer impact, frequency, strategic fit, operational risk, and whether the solution helps many consumers or only creates a one-off path.

How do you avoid overbuilding?

I start with the smallest capability that solves the real workflow pain, then expand based on adoption and repeated needs. Platform teams can overbuild if they do not stay close to users.

14. Hiring and talent bar follow-ups
How do you avoid bias?

I use structured signals, practical scenarios, interviewer calibration, and evidence-based debriefs. I try to avoid vague terms like "culture fit" unless we define the behavior behind it.

What signals matter most?

For senior data engineers, I look for distributed systems fundamentals, data modeling judgement, operational ownership, code quality, tradeoff communication, and ability to mentor others.

Have you rejected a strong technical candidate?

Yes, when the technical depth was strong but ownership or collaboration signals were weak. At senior levels, the wrong behavior can create more drag than the technical skill solves.

How do you onboard hires?

I give them a clear domain, architecture context, operating expectations, a mentor, and one meaningful early deliverable. Onboarding should create confidence without throwing them into chaos.

15. Technical debt follow-ups
How much capacity do you reserve?

It depends on roadmap pressure and operational risk, but I like making capacity explicit rather than hiding debt work inside feature estimates. For high-toil areas, I reserve dedicated capacity until the risk trend changes.

Who decides?

Engineering owns the technical risk assessment, Product helps weigh customer and roadmap impact, and leadership gets involved when the tradeoff changes committed outcomes.

How do you measure debt reduction?

I look at incident reduction, change lead time, manual support effort, onboarding friction, cost reduction, and fewer repeated defects. A cleanup is only valuable if it changes the operating reality.

What debt would you not fix?

I would not fix low-risk debt in a stable area with limited change unless it blocks a strategic goal. Debt prioritization should be disciplined, not emotional.

16. Burnout prevention follow-ups
Did you ever ask people to work late?

Occasionally during genuine incidents or critical cutovers, yes. But I treat that as an exception to recover from, not a planning strategy.

How did stakeholders react?

Most stakeholders responded well when I showed capacity and tradeoffs clearly. It is easier to negotiate scope when you make the cost of hidden work visible.

How do you detect burnout?

I watch for sustained late hours, irritability, lower quality, missed time off, reduced participation, and people saying yes to everything. One-on-ones are where those signals usually surface first.

What tradeoff did you make?

We delayed or descoped some non-critical asks so the team could protect migration and production support. I would rather make a visible tradeoff than silently spend people's energy.

17. Conflict resolution follow-ups
Who was right?

Both sides had valid concerns. One side was optimizing for delivery speed; the other was protecting long-term operability. The right answer combined a framework extension with a short-term adapter.

How did you handle strong personalities?

I moved the discussion to written criteria and assumptions. Strong personalities are easier to manage when the decision is anchored on evidence and agreed principles.

How did you decide?

We evaluated delivery time, operational risk, reuse value, maintenance ownership, and customer impact. Once those criteria were visible, the decision became less personal.

What did you do after?

I clarified ownership, documented the decision, and checked whether the framework improvement helped other teams. A resolved conflict should leave the system cleaner.

18. Reliability follow-ups
What reliability metrics do you track?

Freshness, SLA adherence, incident count, time to detect, time to recover, data quality failures, alert actionability, and repeat-incident rate. For data, correctness is as important as uptime.

How do you reduce alert fatigue?

We remove non-actionable alerts, route alerts to owners, tune thresholds, and page only when there is customer or business risk. Noise trains teams to ignore systems.

How do you handle repeated incidents?

I treat repeated incidents as a system failure, not bad luck. We either automate detection/recovery, redesign the fragile component, or change ownership and support expectations.

How do you choose critical pipelines?

By customer impact, business calendar dependency, downstream fanout, data sensitivity, and recovery complexity. A small pipeline can be critical if many decisions depend on it.

19. Unpopular decision follow-ups
Who disagreed?

Usually the teams closest to the promised date disagreed first because they had commitments to communicate. I tried to make their problem easier by giving a clear rationale and revised path.

Did you get pressure to reverse?

Yes, but I held the line on customer-risk criteria. I was willing to reduce scope, but not to remove validation or rollback requirements for critical data.

How did the team react?

The engineering team appreciated that their concerns were taken seriously. It reinforced that quality concerns should be raised early, not hidden until launch.

Would you make the same call?

Yes. I would communicate the risk earlier, but I would still protect customer trust over a date that the system was not ready to support.

20. Ambiguity follow-ups
How long was discovery?

I prefer a short, timeboxed discovery period. The goal is not to know everything; it is to know enough to choose a pilot, identify major risks, and create decision points.

How did you stop analysis paralysis?

We classified unknowns and converted the most important ones into experiments or stakeholder decisions. If an unknown could only be answered by trying, we used a pilot.

What did the pilot reveal?

It usually reveals hidden dependencies, validation gaps, tooling friction, and whether our migration pattern is practical. That evidence is much better than debating architecture in theory.

How did you communicate uncertainty?

I used clear language: what we know, what we are testing, what decision comes next, and what would change the plan. People can handle uncertainty if it is structured.

21. Blocked by another team follow-ups
Did you escalate?

Only after we had a clear dependency contract and impact statement. Escalation works best when leaders can see the specific decision or tradeoff needed.

What if they still missed?

I would update the plan transparently, activate fallback work, and revisit sequencing. I would not let my team sit idle waiting for a dependency without options.

How did you keep your team productive?

We worked on mocks, validation, fallback handling, documentation, and downstream readiness. Dependency risk should not stop all forward progress.

What did you learn?

I learned to define dependency contracts earlier: what is needed, by when, who owns it, and what happens if it slips. Informal dependencies are where plans get fragile.

22. Communication follow-ups
What tool did you use?

The tool mattered less than the format. We used a single source of truth with status, risks, decisions, owners, and dates, so people stopped relying on scattered meeting notes.

How did you get people to read it?

I made it the source used in meetings and decisions. If a document actually drives decisions, people learn to trust it; if it is extra reporting, they ignore it.

What changed in meetings?

Meetings shifted from task narration to decisions, risks, blockers, and tradeoffs. That gave engineers time back and made stakeholder conversations more useful.

How did you handle bad news?

I surfaced it early with impact, options, and next update timing. Bad news is less damaging when people are not surprised and can still act.

23. Saying no follow-ups
Did the stakeholder escalate?

Sometimes stakeholders escalated because their need was real. When that happened, I stayed focused on the tradeoff and the alternative path, not on defending my original no.

How do you decide priority?

I use customer impact, urgency, strategic fit, reuse potential, operational cost, and existing commitments. Priority should be explainable even when people disagree.

What if the request is urgent?

If it is truly urgent, I will make room, but I still ask what gets deprioritized and how we avoid creating long-term support debt. Urgency should not erase tradeoffs.

How do you avoid being seen as unhelpful?

I say no to the exact path, not to the underlying need. Offering a scalable alternative keeps the relationship constructive.

24. Strategic thinking follow-ups
How did you choose the themes?

I looked for patterns across incidents, roadmap delays, onboarding friction, customer trust gaps, and duplicate engineering work. The themes came from repeated operating pain, not a strategy deck.

How did you fund platform work?

I paired platform improvements with roadmap work where possible and reserved capacity for high-risk reliability or cost items. Platform work is easier to fund when tied to delivery, reliability, or cost outcomes.

What did you deprioritize?

We deprioritized some one-off pipeline work and non-critical enhancements that did not align with reuse, trust, or reliability. Strategy requires saying no to reasonable but low-leverage work.

How did you communicate strategy to engineers?

I translated it into engineering decisions: use common frameworks, improve dataset ownership, reduce toil, design for operability, and build reusable capabilities. Strategy has to show up in daily choices.
